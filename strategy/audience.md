# Audience

## Who they are

Founders, indie builders, and marketing operators in noisy AI-era markets. People who are already creating content or products but aren't getting predictable attention or traction.

## The 7 dimensions of their worldview

The simulator's Step 1 (Build Reader) lifts from these 7 dimensions. The Writer's draft frontmatter carries the rendered Reader field. The Strategist must keep all 7 dimensions in mind when generating the brief — none are optional.

### 1. Beliefs — what they currently believe to be true

- "Good products should 'naturally' get traction."
- "Consistency alone will eventually work."
- "More content = more growth."
- "Virality is unpredictable and mostly luck."
- "I just need to write better content / build a better product / find the right channel."
- "If I keep shipping, the audience will come."

### 2. Frustrations — what they are tired of

- Random results: "I post 3 times a week, sometimes it hits, mostly it doesn't."
- Inconsistent metrics: "Some posts go viral, others die. I can't tell why."
- Tired advice: "Everyone says 'just post more consistently' or 'make better content' or 'focus on storytelling' without giving the actual mechanics."
- Generic growth hacks: "Every growth hack is the same. None of them explain the system."
- The content treadmill: "I'm spending more time creating content than building the product."
- The hope-and-pray cycle: "I keep hoping this post will hit, and it doesn't."

### 3. Constraints — what blocks them

- **Time:** They are builders first, content creators second. They have hours per week, not days.
- **Money:** They are pre-product-market-fit or early-stage. They cannot afford to hire an agency or a content team.
- **Skill:** They are technical, not marketing-trained. They don't have a copywriter's instinct.
- **Audience:** They have no audience or a tiny one. They cannot rely on distribution that requires scale.
- **Attention:** Their own attention is split between building, marketing, customer support, and life. They cannot context-switch into "creator mode" easily.

### 4. Identity tension — who they are vs who they need to be

- They are **builders**, not creators. The "creator" identity feels like putting on a costume.
- They want to be seen as **the technical expert**, not the "content guy."
- They are skeptical of **marketing** as a discipline. It feels manipulative, not authentic.
- They want distribution that **respects their work**, not distribution that demands they perform.
- The tension: they need to ship content to grow, but they don't want to become a content creator. They want content to be a byproduct of building.

### 5. Confusion state — what they don't have language for

- They cannot articulate the difference between "content quality" and "content placement."
- They don't have a mental model for **attention flows** — they think in terms of "posts" and "platforms," not "where attention already exists" and "how to enter it."
- They confuse **distribution** with **promotion.** Distribution is where the post lives; promotion is telling people about it. They are stuck on promotion.
- They don't know that **time-anchored** (when you post) and **placement-anchored** (where you post) are the two biggest levers, not the post itself.
- They cannot tell the difference between a **session** and a **post.** They think a post is something they write separately from their work. They don't realize their work session is the source.

### 6. Language style — words and register they actually use

Lifted from audience.md "They will pay attention when" + their own quotes:

- "Engineering attention"
- "Attention flows"
- "Distribution system"
- "Placement"
- "Real-time mechanics"
- "Sustained on-page engagement"
- "Predictable traffic"
- "Repeatable loops"
- "System-based posting"
- "Engineered, not hoped for"
- "Byproduct of building"
- "Compounding distribution"
- "Production system"
- "Asset in attention flows"

The ICP speaks in **systems language**, not **marketing language.** They think in flows, loops, mechanics, systems, production — not in "vibes", "reach", "engagement bait", "viral hooks."

### 7. Internal monologue — the questions they ask themselves

- "Why are my numbers flat? Am I writing the wrong content?"
- "I shipped 3 things this quarter. Which one moved the needle?"
- "Is consistency enough? Or am I missing something?"
- "I keep refining the engine. Why isn't the system working?"
- "Where is the attention already? How do I find it?"
- "Am I failing at marketing, or at distribution?"
- "I built something useful. Why doesn't anyone see it?"
- "If I post the same thing on a different channel, would the numbers change?"
- "What if the bottleneck isn't the post, it's where the post lives?"
- "I keep tweaking the visible parts. The invisible parts are still broken."

## What they want

A repeatable way to get attention without relying on luck, virality, or big existing audiences. Distribution that feels systematic, not random.

## Why they are stuck

They still treat marketing as "posting content" instead of "engineering attention flows." They don't know how to consistently enter existing demand streams or convert attention into structured systems.

## What they will pay attention to

They see real proof that attention can be engineered, not hoped for. When they see numbers, experiments, and distribution mechanics broken down in real time — not theory, but systems that actually move traffic and retention.
